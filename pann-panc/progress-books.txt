Progress.ai public publication findings and PANC notes
Date: 2026-06-12

Scope
-----
I reviewed the public Progress.ai team, publications, PANC, PANN, tests, and
patent pages, plus the public Springer metadata for their 2026 AIR-RES paper.
Springer exposes the abstract, references, keywords, authors, and DOI metadata
for the PANC paper, but not the full chapter text without institutional or paid
access. Progress.ai states the same access limitation on its publication page.

Core PANC/PANN publications found
---------------------------------

1. "Comparative Recognition Technology for Artificial Intelligence"
   URL: https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-032-13056-3_17
   DOI: https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-032-13056-3_17
   First online: 2026-01-10.
   Authors listed publicly by Springer: Boris Zlotin, Alla Zusman,
   Vladimir Proseanic, Svetlana Visnepolschi, James Cook, Anatol Guin,
   Vladimir Matsenko, Oleg Gafurov.
   Publicly visible content: abstract, references, keywords, and metadata.
   PANC-relevant public details:
   - Uses a "binary comparison format" (BCF) for recognition/classification.
   - Uses structured comparison libraries for similarity-based recognition.
   - Claims reduced computational complexity and recognition time.
   - Mentions digital and analog implementations, including medical diagnostics.
   - Mentions a "PANC_Platform" program for recognition and cross-classification.
   - Keywords include Associative Libraries, Explainable AI, Coefficient of
     Similarity, Comparison, Recognition.
   Missing from public abstract: exact BCF encoding, coefficient-of-similarity
   formula, analogue library construction algorithm, and implementation details.

2. "Peer-Reviewed Recognition of PANC Technology"
   URL: https://progress.ai/peer-reviewed-recognition-of-panc-technology/
   Progress.ai announcement for two AIR-RES 2025 Springer papers.
   PANC-relevant details:
   - Reviewer quote describes structured comparison libraries plus software or
     analog comparators as the central alternative to traditional AI training.
   - Progress.ai says Springer links expose abstracts/references only; full
     papers require institutional or paid access.

3. "PANC AI Technology"
   URL: https://progress.ai/panc-ai-technology/
   PANC-relevant details:
   - PANC is explicitly comparator-centric, not gradient-trained.
   - Recognition is framed as retrieving closest analogues from a similarity
     library and transferring relevant information.
   - Comparisons are described as binary/similarity operations over reference
     libraries, potentially in parallel.
   - Claims little/no traditional training, smaller matrices, and in many
     operations a single multiplication.

4. "What is a Neuromorphic Comparator?"
   URL: https://progress.ai/what-is-a-neuromorphic-comparator/
   Dialogue by Anatoly Guin and Boris Zlotin, 2025-10-09.
   PANC-relevant details:
   - PANC is not an ANN.
   - It compares a holistic input image against a rich image library.
   - It returns similarity scores/ranked analogues rather than hidden weights.
   - It does not sequentially scan the library; they claim simultaneous access.
   - "No training": the first replacement is a static image library.
   - Each PANC "neuron" stores a single image independently; images can be
     added, removed, or modified without retraining the whole system.
   - This supports our PANC-like analogue library direction, but not enough to
     reconstruct their exact proprietary comparator.

5. "Energy efficiency of the PANC technology"
   URL: https://progress.ai/energy-efficiency-of-the-panc-technology/
   Dialogue by Anatoly Guin and Boris Zlotin, 2025-10-16.
   PANC-relevant details:
   - Repeats that PANC requires no training.
   - Claims PANC has a matrix multiplication, but matrices are thousands of
     times smaller and there is only a single multiplication.

6. "Chart recognition"
   URL: https://progress.ai/chart-recognition/
   Dialogue by Anatoly Guin and Boris Zlotin, 2025-10-31.
   PANC-relevant details:
   - Preprocessing is domain-specific. Faces, charts, landscapes, portraits,
     radiographs, etc. need different preparation.
   - For ECG/charts, background dominated scoring until they removed it.
   - Claims they do not need 100,000 images; around 100-300 processed images
     may suffice in some domains.
   - Describes semi-automatic library/class construction: pick one example,
     run comparator, inspect top matches, discard wrong examples, form class.
   - Describes positive-feedback library growth: recognized new images can be
     added to the library.
   - Describes fast transform search: scale, tilt, rotation, etc.

7. "Priorities for applying PANC"
   URL: https://progress.ai/priorities-for-applying-panc/
   Dialogue by Anatoly Guin and Boris Zlotin, 2025-10-24.
   PANC-relevant details:
   - Describes priority domains: drones, chemical/reactor control, automobiles,
     medicine/diagnostics, epidemic monitoring, and scattered-information
     analysis.
   - Repeats that PANC does not need training in the neural-network sense.
   - Gives a concrete library-growth concept for autonomous driving: start with
     a small local library, observe human drivers, add road situations, and
     propagate newly discovered situations across networked cars.
   - Repeats the "each neuron is an independent piece of knowledge" idea:
     library entries can be added, removed, modified, or partitioned without
     disturbing the rest of the system.
   - Claims the key shift is "computation to comparison", especially for large
     scattered-data problems.

8. "Market for PANC"
   URL: https://progress.ai/market-for-panc/
   Dialogue by Anatoly Guin and Boris Zlotin, 2025-11-13.
   PANC-relevant details:
   - Mostly market positioning, not algorithmic detail.
   - Useful only as corroboration that they see PANN/PANC as simple, inexpensive,
     scalable intelligence embedded in vehicles, homes, robots, advisors, and
     education/entertainment systems.

9. "On the Mona Lisa and a scientists' stereotype"
   URL: https://progress.ai/on-the-mona-lisa-and-a-scientists-stereotype/
   Dialogue by Anatoly Guin and Boris Zlotin, 2025-11-06.
   PANC-relevant details:
   - They claim better recognition with tiny machine artifacts than with
     large human-looking images in some cases.
   - Specific public hint: reduce to 32 x 32 pixels and ten grayscale levels.
   - They explicitly warn that 6 MB images may be worse for their system than
     32 x 32 versions.
   - This is the strongest public clue that our next PANC work should test a
     low-resolution quantized representation, not only richer handcrafted
     image descriptors.

10. "Precursors of Artificial Intelligence"
   URL: https://progress.ai/precursors-of-artificial-intelligence/
   Dialogue by Anatoly Guin and Boris Zlotin, 2025-11-23.
   PANC-relevant details:
   - They cite Gibson, Gregory, Marr, and Gleyzer as conceptual precursors.
   - They describe memory as many portraits/analogues and recognition as finding
     most similar stored items and transferring information.
   - They claim use cases including cell nuclei, radiographs, and faces.
   - They claim the comparator compares one image against thousands or millions
     in parallel, and that throughput depends on cores/computer power.

11. "How PANN and PANC Will Affect the AI Industry"
   URL: https://progress.ai/how-pann-and-panc-affect-ai-industry/
   Dialogue by Anatoly Guin and Boris Zlotin, 2025-11-28.
   PANC-relevant details:
   - PANN is described as a single-layer neural network.
   - PANC is described as an associative neuromorphic comparator, not a neural
     network.
   - They say PANN came first; then they asked why it worked better and moved
     toward recognition by comparison with library images.
   - They also say PANC is still immature and should be hybridized with mature
     conventional software.

12. "AI-Empowered Creative Problem Solving"
    URL: https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-032-13056-3_28
    Progress.ai lists it as the second AIR-RES 2025 paper, pages 396-412.
    I could not read the full chapter publicly. Based on Progress.ai's
    description, it is more about AI-assisted creative problem solving than
    PANC implementation.

PANN publications found
-----------------------

1. "Analog and Digital Modeling of a Scalable Neural Network"
   URL: https://progress.ai/analog-digital-modeling-scalable-neural-network/
   Authors: D. Pescianschi, A. Boudichevskaia, B. Zlotin, V. Proseanic.
   PANN-relevant details:
   - P-network/PANN uses multiple weights per synapse.
   - Corrective weight selection depends on incoming signal intensity.
   - Output can be a simple sum; sigmoid is not required.
   - Training corrects selected weights from direct and retrograde/expected
     signals.
   - Digital recognition and training can be written as matrix operations.
   - Test section says inputs=1000, outputs=20, images/records=500-7000; around
     7000 records gave a 3250x speed comparison against IBM SPSS Statistics 22.

2. "Main Principles of the General Theory of Neural Network with Internal Feedback"
   URL: https://progress.ai/main-principles-general-theory-neural-network-internal-feedback/
   Author: D. Pescianschi.
   PANN-relevant details:
   - Biological/internal-feedback motivation for local learning.
   - Training is per-neuron/local and selected mediators/weights depend on input
     signal level.
   - One epoch may be repeated until accuracy/convergence threshold is reached.

3. "Fast trained neural network"
   URL: https://progress.ai/fast-trained-neural-network/
   PANN-relevant details:
   - Describes PANN classes/objects: PNet, InputSignal, NeuronUnit, Synapse.
   - Synapse contains an array of corrective weights.
   - InputSignal stores min/max signal values, interval count, and interval
     width.
   - Training sums coefficient-weight products, calculates correction, and
     applies coefficient-scaled correction to selected/corresponding weights.
   - Reports an Iris comparison where PANN training speed was 1 ms vs 22,141 ms
     for a TensorFlow DNN at comparable error.

Progress.ai tests page notes
----------------------------
URL: https://progress.ai/tests/

The page text I fetched includes:

- "Training Time and Error Benchmarking"
- "Target error: 0.02 (Mean Squared Error)"
- Progress PANN frame1:
  - 1 epoch: error 0.058916, training time 343 ms.
  - 5 epochs: error 0.018097, training time 640 ms.
  - 10 epochs: error 0.018097, training time 640 ms.
- It also says "Tested training set: 30,000 images" near that section.
- A separate IBM SPSS comparison says:
  - PANN: 7,000 images, 4s.
  - IBM SPSS Statistics 22: 7,000 images, 3h 43m.
  - Training advantage factor: 13,400s / 4s = 3,350.

Important interpretation:

- The 0.018 value is Mean Squared Error, not necessarily classification error
  or image-recognition accuracy.
- The 7000-image/4-second claim appears in a PANN training-speed comparison,
  not in the PANC no-training analogue-library comparator material.
- Their older PANN paper states the test had 1000 inputs and 20 outputs with
  500-7000 records/images, so "image" may mean vectorized records/samples in a
  compact format, not JPEG decoding plus natural-image feature extraction.
- This is not directly comparable to our cat/dog image-folder benchmarks.

Does our dataset need to be larger?
-----------------------------------
Probably not as the main issue.

Progress.ai's own PANC-oriented dialogue says some domains may need only
100-300 specifically processed examples, while a 1000-image initial library can
bootstrap larger library growth with user/specialist feedback. For our current
PetImages experiments, 1500 training images and 3000 evaluation images is
already large enough to test whether the representation/comparator direction is
right.

The stronger conclusion is that our representation and preprocessing are still
probably wrong/incomplete relative to their public PANC hints:

- Need low-resolution direct artifacts: 32 x 32 grayscale, around ten levels.
- Need background/foreground or object-region handling.
- Need domain-specific preparation for cats/dogs.
- Need similarity over analogue libraries, not a conventional trainable hidden
  model.
- Need class/library construction, top-match inspection, and positive-feedback
  library growth.
- Need fast transform search: crop/scale/tilt/rotation variants.
- Need a BCF-like compact representation and a coefficient-of-similarity
  scoring family; exact formula remains undisclosed publicly.

Recommended next implementation direction
-----------------------------------------

1. Add a "panc-lowres" image encoder:
   - center/object crop option;
   - resize to 32 x 32;
   - grayscale quantization to 10 levels;
   - optional bit-plane/BCF-like packing.

2. Add similarity metrics designed for this representation:
   - exact-level match ratio;
   - absolute-difference similarity over 10-level cells;
   - bit-plane Hamming/Jaccard;
   - block-wise weighted similarity.

3. Add transform search for PANC:
   - center crop, square pad, scale variants, horizontal flip;
   - later: small rotations/tilts.

4. Add library builder:
   - rank top matches;
   - write top-k match sheets;
   - allow selected/accepted analogues to become class library entries.

5. Treat PANN and PANC separately in reports:
   - PANN: training epochs, MSE/error, interval-coded weights.
   - PANC: no training in the ANN sense; library construction, encoding, and
     similarity/ranking quality.

Bottom line
-----------
Public Progress.ai material confirms our general direction for a PANC-like
baseline: analogue libraries, compact image artifacts, similarity scoring, and
explainable top matches. It does not expose enough public detail to clone their
exact PANC comparator. The most actionable new clue is not "use more images";
it is "try much smaller, quantized, domain-preprocessed images and compare them
as a library."
